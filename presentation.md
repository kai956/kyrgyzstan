Content:
The Kyrgyz people originated in the Yenisei River valley in southern Siberia. They established the Kyrgyz Khaganate, which became a significant power in Central Asia. Archaeological evidence shows they had advanced metalworking skills and a well-organized society.
Key Points:

3. –¢–µ–Ω–≥—Ä–∏–∞–Ω—Å—Ç–≤–æ (Tengrism)
Content:
Tengrism is the ancient belief system of the Kyrgyz and other Central Asian peoples, centered on Tengri (the Sky God) and Umay (the Earth Goddess). This shamanic religion emphasized harmony with nature and the spiritual connection between sky, earth, and humanity.
Core Beliefs:

Tengri (Eternal Blue Sky) - supreme deity
Umay (Mother Earth) - goddess of fertility
Ancestor worship and nature spirits
Shamans as mediators between worlds
Sacred mountains and natural sites
Ritual ceremonies and animal sacrifices

Practices:

Spring and autumn equinox celebrations
Horse sacrifice rituals
Ovoo (stone cairn) worship at mountain passes
Divination and healing rituals

Modern Influence: Elements survive in folklore, traditional ceremonies, and cultural practices
Visual Elements: Traditional symbols, ritual objects, sacred mountain imagery
4. –†—É–Ω–∏—á–µ—Å–∫–∞—è –ü–∏—Å—å–º–µ–Ω–Ω–æ—Å—Ç—å (Runic Script)
Content:
The Kyrgyz used the Orkhon-Yenisei runic alphabet, also known as the Old Turkic script. This writing system was used from the 8th to 10th centuries and is found on stone monuments, known as stelae, throughout Central Asia and southern Siberia.
Characteristics:

38 characters representing consonants and vowels
Written right to left or left to right
Carved on stone monuments and rocks
Used for memorial inscriptions, dedications, and records
Similar to ancient Turkic runes

Famous Examples:

Yenisei inscriptions in Siberia
Memorial stones for khagans (rulers)
Trade markings and property labels

Connection to Japan: Some scholars note similarities between Turkic runes and ancient Japanese characters, suggesting possible cultural exchange through the Silk Road.
Visual Elements: Runic inscriptions, animated writing demonstration, stone monuments
5. –Æ—Ä—Ç–∞ (Yurt) - The Nomadic Home
Content:
The yurt is the traditional portable dwelling of Kyrgyz nomads, perfectly adapted to the nomadic lifestyle and mountainous terrain. It can be assembled in 1-2 hours and disassembled just as quickly, making it ideal for seasonal migrations.
Structure:

Wooden frame: collapsible lattice walls (kerege)
Roof poles (uuk) radiating from center
Crown wheel (tunduk) at the top - symbol on Kyrgyz flag
Felt covering for insulation
Decorated with traditional patterns

Cultural Significance:

Right side traditionally for women
Left side for men and guests
Place of honor opposite the door
Decorated interior showing family status
Center for social and family life

Symbolism: The tunduk (crown) represents the universe, family unity, and the connection between earth and sky
Visual Elements: 3D rotating yurt model, construction animation, interior panorama

Rules:

Dead goat or calf used (30-40 kg)
Points scored by throwing carcass into opponent's "tai" (goal circle)
Extremely rough and dangerous
Displays horsemanship, strength, teamwork

Cultural Significance:

Ancient training for cavalry warfare
Tests of manhood and skill
Major events at festivals and weddings
National pride (Kyrgyz team dominates internationally)
